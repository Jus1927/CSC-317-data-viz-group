What is drawdown and why does it matter?

Climate change is often presented is an intimidating and nearly intractable problem. And to be clear, it certainly is a problem that should be taken seriously.

Currently, the earth is releasing more greenhouse gases into the atmosphere than it is absorbing back into the land and oceans. The impact of adding more greenhouse gases or GHG can be compared to adding more and more layers of blankets around the earth. GHG reflect infrared radiation back towards the earth as heat. As the greenhouse gases continue to the impacts of the trapped heat will become more impactful and costly. Some of the impacts of climate change include raising sea levels that can displace coastal communities, more frequent and intense heat waves, and more frequent extreme weather events such as droughts, floods, and wet bulb events.

However, understanding the concept of drawdown can help clarify where action needs to take place. Drawdown represents the point in time in which the greenhouse gas sinks are absorbing as much or more GHG as the amount of emissions being released by GHG sources. By shrinking GHG sources and increasing GHG sinks, we can reach drawdown.

Greenhouse Gas Emission Sources

Currently, GHG sources can be sorted into six major categories which include:

  1. Electricity Production: 25%
  2. Food Agriculture & Land Use: 24%
  3. Industry: 21%
  4. Transportation: 14%
  5. Buildings: 6%
  6. Other Energy Related Emissions: 10%

Solutions to Shrink Greenhouse Gas Emission Sources

There are existing solutions for each of the six categories of sources listed above. One of the mot impactful solutions is reducing food waste. The second largest source of GHG emissions is from agriculture and land usd, and it is estimated that 30-40% of the food produced from this system of agriculture in the United States is wasted. Reducing food waste starting from the production of food, transport, preparation, consumption, and disposal is essential to minimizing the negative impacts of climate change.

One of the solutions with largest predicted reduction in carbon emissions for elictricity production is the creation of onshore wind turbines with an estimated potential reduction in emissions by 46.95 to 143.56 gigatons of CO2.

To read about more of the most impactful solutions to reducing these sources, you can read through Project Drawdown's Table of Solutions.

Greenhouse Gas Natural Sinks

Natural sinks are extremely important as they absorb CO2 and other GHG from the atmosphere. Taking care of our natural sinks and helping them thrive can help minimize the negative impacts from climate change.

There are two large categories of natural sinks which are land sinks and coastal & ocean sinks. Land sinks can be further divided into things like forests, wetlands, soil, peatlands, and even urban green spaces.

Ways to Expand Greenhouse Gas Sinks

There are existing solutions for both expanding coast & ocean sinks as well as for expanding land sinks. However, the projected impact of the solutions for land sinks is significantly larger.

The solution with the largest predicted impact for expanding coastal and ocean sinks is seaweed farming; this has an projected reducting in emissions by 2.5 to 4.7 gigatons of CO2.

The most impactful solutions for expanding land sinks are projected to be much more impactful, which is partially because of how poorly we are currently treating our existing land sinks. Tropical forest restoration has a potential increase in emission absorbtion by 54.5 to 85.1 gigatons of CO2 annually.

Some other notable solutions for expanding land sinks include planting trees on degraded land, improved agricultural practices such as crop rotation and tree intercropping, as well as temperate forest restoration.
